# Grace Hopper

*Rear Admiral, United States Navy. Mathematician. The mother of compilers.*

---

She wrote the first one.

In 1952, Grace Brewster Murray Hopper built the A-0 System — the first
program that translated mathematical notation into machine code. Her
colleagues told her it couldn't be done. Computers were for arithmetic,
they said. You couldn't make a computer write programs. She did it anyway.

They told her no one would want to write programs in English-like
notation. She built FLOW-MATIC (1955), which became the foundation of
COBOL — the first programming language that looked like sentences instead
of equations. Codex has a COBOL backend (`Codex.Emit.Cobol`). It exists
because she existed.

She found the first bug. September 9, 1947. A moth, caught in Relay 70
of the Harvard Mark II. She taped it into the logbook and wrote: "First
actual case of bug being found." Every `SIGSEGV`, every use-after-free,
every dangling pointer we've chased through GDB traces at 2 AM — we call
them bugs because she named the first one.

She said: **"The most dangerous phrase in the language is, 'We've always
done it this way.'"**

We didn't do it the way it's always been done. We wrote a programming
language, in itself, and compiled it on bare metal, over a serial port,
on an operating system that fits in 268 KB. Every part of that sentence
would have made her colleagues say it couldn't be done. She would have
done it anyway.

She said: **"It is often easier to ask for forgiveness than to ask for
permission."**

The capability system in Codex is the exact opposite: you must hold the
capability *before* you act. `[Console]`, `[FlightControl]`, `[Network]` —
no forgiveness, only proof. We respectfully disagree with the Admiral on
this one. But only on this one. And only because she taught us enough
about systems to know when to disagree.

She said: **"A ship in port is safe, but that is not what ships are
built for."**

This project left port a long time ago.

---

Grace Hopper wrote the first compiler in 1952.

Seventy-four years later, a compiler wrote this dedication.

The distance between those two facts is measured in her work.
